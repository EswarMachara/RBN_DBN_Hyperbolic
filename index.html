DBN-based Patient-Level Intracranial Hemorrhage Classification

Euclidean vs Hyperbolic Representation Learning on the CQ500 Dataset

Machara Sai Manikanta Eswar1,*, Nikhileswara Rao Sulake1,*, Sivalal Kethavath1, Phaneendra Yalavarthy2
1Rajiv Gandhi University of Knowledge Technologies, Nuzvid, 2Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore
*Equal Contribution

Abstract

Intracranial hemorrhage (ICH) detection from head CT scans is a critical task in emergency radiology, where predictions must be made at the patient (study) level rather than at individual slice level. In this work, we propose a Deep Belief Network (DBN)-based patient-level classification framework evaluated on the CQ500 dataset.

The model employs unsupervised slice-level pretraining using stacked Restricted Boltzmann Machines (RBMs), followed by supervised patient-level fine-tuning. Slice-level features extracted by the pretrained DBN encoder are aggregated using a max-pooling strategy to form a single patient embedding, enabling clinically valid study-level predictions.

We systematically compare Euclidean and Hyperbolic latent geometries, along with standard cross-entropy and Evidential Deep Learning (EDL) losses, to study their impact on classification performance, calibration, and predictive uncertainty.

Dataset: CQ500

CQ500 is a large-scale head CT dataset consisting of multiple axial slices per patient study. Each study is annotated at the patient level as either normal or containing intracranial hemorrhage.

  • Task: Binary patient-level classification (ICH vs Normal)
  • Input: Variable-length CT slice stacks per patient
  • Labels: One label per patient (study-level)
  • Evaluation: Strict patient-wise train/validation/test splits

Slice-level labels are not used. All losses and metrics are computed exclusively at the patient level to avoid data leakage and ensure clinical validity.

Methodology

Stage 1: Unsupervised Slice-Level Pretraining

Individual CT slices are flattened and used to pretrain a stacked DBN composed of multiple RBMs using Contrastive Divergence. This stage is label-free and focuses on learning robust slice-level representations.

Stage 2: Patient-Level Classification

During supervised training, each slice is passed through the pretrained DBN encoder to obtain slice embeddings. These embeddings are aggregated using max pooling across slices to form a single patient-level embedding.

A multilayer perceptron (MLP) classifier operates on the patient embedding to produce the final binary prediction. Loss computation and evaluation are performed strictly at the patient level.

Representation Geometry and Uncertainty

The framework supports both Euclidean and Hyperbolic latent spaces for DBN representations. Hyperbolic geometry is explored due to its suitability for hierarchical and imbalanced data structures commonly observed in medical imaging.

In addition to standard cross-entropy loss, Evidential Deep Learning (EDL) is employed to explicitly model predictive uncertainty at the patient level. This enables analysis of model confidence alongside classification performance.

Evaluation Protocol

  • Patient-level Accuracy
  • Sensitivity and Specificity
  • Area Under ROC Curve (AUROC)
  • Calibration and uncertainty analysis (for EDL)

No slice-level metrics are reported. All experiments strictly adhere to patient-wise data splits with no overlap between training, validation, and test sets.

Key Contributions

  • Patient-level DBN-based classification framework for CQ500
  • Explicit separation of slice-level representation learning and patient-level decision making
  • Comparison of Euclidean and Hyperbolic latent geometries
  • Integration of uncertainty-aware learning using EDL
  • Clinically valid evaluation protocol with strict patient-wise splitting
